Inspiration

One of my favorite card games as a child was war and I vividly remember the fancy artwork on the large cards of the war deck that I owned. Though this program simply uses integers, it reminded me of a pastime and art that brought much joy to my childhood.

What it does

This program automates the game of war. In brief, players are dealt a hand and flip over the top card in their hand. The winner takes all of the cards from that round. In the event of a tie, the two temporary winners burn a card and play their next card to decide the final winner. If at any point you run out of cards, you are out of the game. The game continues until only one player is left.

How I built it

I simply used Notepad++ and the command window on Windows to build this project. Structurally, each player is represented by a List pointer within a dynamically allocated array. Each list pointer points to a Linked List that contains the players current hand of cards. As cards are played, they are redistributed to the winning players hand as necessary.

Challenges I ran into

The first main challenge was accounting for malloc errors. Since I wanted to dynamically allocate everything, if malloc failed at any point all previously allocated memory had to be freed. This was often very difficult considering how many linkages I had within the base structure of the data type anyways, so to account for every possible scenario was difficult.

The other challenge that I ran into was the event of multiple ties. In the event of a tie, the card after the tie is burned and the next card is played to determine the winner. Though I accounted for a single tie within the program, it was going to be extremely difficult to recursively loop through n-tiebreakers until a distinct winner was finally determined. This is because there could be multiple ties (a 3 way tie) and thus the discarded cards all had to be pushed onto a stack; if at any time someone ran out of cards, they're out of the game but there still may be 2 players fighting for the distinct win. All in all, there a lot of edge cases but the specific case of a tie after a tie was too difficult to recursively implement with my data structures in the given 36 hour timeframe.
